— France, 1600 AD —

After Isaac Newton's invention of Calculus in the 17th century, mathematicians began searching for a way to calculate differential equations using mechanical mechanisms. Several descriptions of such hypothetical devices had been published after Newton's death, but it wasn't until 1836, that a physical device was successfully constructed. A French mathematician named Gaspard-Gustave de Coriolis was the inventor of the first device which used wheel-and-disc mechanisms [6] to integrate differential equations. Coriolis discovered that if two rear wheels of a differential (a set of wheels with different rotational velocities) are turned, the drive shaft (responsible for transferring torque) will compute the average of the left and right wheels. Rear wheels can then be chosen in a specific ratio that the device can use to preform multiplication and division. For example, a wheel ratio of 1:2 enables multiplication by 2.[7] Coriolis called his device the Differential Analyser. Although its computational output is seemingly simple by today's standards, it was revolutionary because in Calculus, multiplication establishes the technique for integrating a constant function. Although Coriolis' invention isn't exactly ancient, the discovery of this ability to obtain solutions for differential equations using mechanical components marked an important milestone for the application of differential calculus.

The invention of Coriolis' Differential Analyser also had profound effects on technologies outside the field of mathematics. Britain's development of the bouncing bomb used in WWII, for instance, utilised the differential analyser to calculate the path that bombs took while "bouncing" across water and avoiding torpedo nets.[8] The many practicle applications of this device established it as an essential mathematical tool and the first advanced computing device to be used operationally.
