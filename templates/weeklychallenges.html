Ismail al-Jazari was a Muslim mathematician, mechanical engineer, and scholar born in the 12th century. Considered the "father of robotics", he invented several ground-breaking devices over his lifetime, and accelerated the advancement of mechanical technology of his time. His most well-known invention was a redesign of the water clock. Based on the previous concept of a Pseudo-Archimedes, Al-Jazari called his device the Castle clock, which had the ability to perform various, relatively complex functions.[4]

The Castle Clock is not only visually complex. The clock could perform tasks beyond that of just time-keeping. It displayed the orbits of the sun and moon, and had the ability to accept instructions,[5] so that the length of each day could be set in order to adjust for the annual changing of the seasons. The clock sat about 11 feet high, and displayed the zodiac,lunar, and solar orbits with a pointer traveling along a gateway. The pointer was moved by a hidden cart. Another interesting fact about the device is that it featured robotic musicians playing music periodically. The timekeeping abilities, which controlled the functioning of all these features, was ultimately determined by a float that lifted or sank in response to a moving water wheel.

Because of its ability to take instructions, the Castle Clock is considered to be the very first programmable analog computer.
